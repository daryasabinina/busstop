    Is ConsumerReports.org Free for Magazine Subscribers?

    Consumer Reports magazine and ConsumerReports.org are two separate subscriptions. As a magazine subscriber, you do not automatically receive our online service.  However, Consumer Reports magazine subscribers are entitled to a discount on an annual subscription to ConsumerReports.org.

    Stop Auto Renewal to ConsumerReports.org

    If you are an annual subscriber and would like to stop future auto-renewals, please visit the "My Account" area of our site and sign in.  After signing in, you will see a link called "Cancel Subscription."  If you click this, you will be presented with three options:

    -  Go ahead and cancel now
    -  Do not cancel my subscription
    -  Keep access until my subscription ends

    This last option will allow you to remain active on our site for the full term you paid for, but you will not renew automatically at the end of the term.  You would need to go through the subscription process and provide payment information again if you should decide to use our service after your expiration date.

    If you are a monthly subscriber and you would like to stop future monthly auto-renewals, please visit the "My Account" area of our site and sign in.  After signing in, you will see a link called "Cancel Subscription."  If you click this, you will be presented with two options:

    -          Do not cancel my subscription

    -          Cancel My Access

    The second option will stop future monthly renewals, but you will continue to have access to the site for the month you have paid for.

    Unable to Login to Website

    If you have an active subscription and you know your username and password, but cannot login to ConsumerReports.org , below we've listed possible reasons below, as well as suggestions for what to do:

    BROWSERS: Make sure you are using the latest version of Internet Explorer (7.0 or better) or Firefox (3.6)


    To find out what version of Internet Explorer you are using:
    1. Open a new browser and on the Tools bar click on "Help".
    2. Click on "About Internet Explorer".
    3. A new window will pop-up that will provide you with the version you are using.

    To find out what version of Firefox you are using:
    1. Open a new browser and on the Tools bar click on "Help".
    2. Click on "About".
    3. A new window will pop-up that will provide you with the version you are using.

    Support for the different browsers are located below:
    Internet Explorer support is located at: http://support.microsoft.com/ie6
    Firefox support is located at: - http://en-us.www.mozilla.com/en-US/support/

    JAVASCRIPT: JavaScript is used to validate forms, text boxes, detect browser types, create cookies and to add functionality to online products. ConsumerReports.org users should verify that JavaScript and the related applets are enabled.

    Internet Explorer 7.0 or Better
    1. Open Internet Explorer.
    2. Select Internet Options from the Tools menu.
    3. In Internet Options dialog box select the Security tab.
    4. Click Custom level button at bottom. The Security settings dialog box will pop up.
    5. Under Scripting category enable Active Scripting, Allow paste options via script and Scripting of

    Java applets.
    6. Click OK twice to close out.
    7. Click the Refresh button.

    FireFox 3.6
    1. Open FireFox.
    2. Select Options from the Tools menu.
    3. Click the Content tab5.
    4. Check both Enable Java and Enable JavaScript.
    5. Close the window.
    6. Click the Reload button.

    FIREWALL: You are using a computer at your place of work, which is behind a "firewall". Firewalls are generally set-up by a company's technical or computer department to help protect and secure its systems. You can ask your technical or computer department if your computer is behind a firewall. If it is, ask them to configure the firewall so that you can access the subscriber area of our site. Also, make sure that your firewall is not blocking access to our secure pages which need port 443 open.
    For more information on Windows XP firewalls, and how they can be disabled, please visit the following URL:
    www.microsoft.com/windowsxp/using/networking/learnmore/icf.mspx

    For all other firewalls please refer to the Help feature on your firewall software.

    SSL CONNECTIONS: The browser and/or online service you are using doesn't handle secure connections using the SSL ("secure socket layer") encryption standard. There are some browsers that cannot handle the SSL connections (e.g. MAC users with AOL browsers), and others that do not handle them reliably (e.g. WebTV). The only solution is to use another browser (Internet Explorer 7.0 or higher, etc.).

    SSL NOT TURNED ON: Your browser handles SSL ("secure socket layer") connections, but this capability is not turned "ON". When you subscribe, and also when you Login, you are transmitting private personal information, (e.g. Username, Password, etc). SSL connections safeguard this information. To find out how to turn the connections "ON", use your browser's HELP feature, or contact the manufacturer of your browser directly for guidance (by phone or online).

    COOKIES: You are not accepting the "cookie" that is sent during Login. By accepting the cookie, your Username and Password are kept intact throughout your ConsumerReports.org session so that you are not repeatedly asked to enter them. To find out how to have your browser automatically accept the "cookie", use your browser's HELP feature, or contact the manufacturer of your browser directly for guidance (by phone or online). You will also need to allow session and persistent cookies for our site.

    To allow session and persistent cookies:

    For Internet Explorer users:
    1. Open a new browser and go to Tools - Internet Options
    2. Click on the Privacy tab and click on the Advanced button.
    3. Click on the box "Override automatic cookie handling".
    4. Click on the box "Always allow session cookies" and press OK.

    Previously stored cookies can also interfere with your login. (Very Important: Please note that if you delete all your cookies, this will delete all usernames and passwords you have saved for other websites. Therefore, next time you visit a site that you used to automatically login, you will have to enter your username and password).
    Deleting cookies (Windows PC):

    If you use Internet Explorer:
    1. Go to the C: drive
    2. Click on "Documents and Settings"
    3. Click on "User" folder
    4. Click on "Cookies" folder. Then, selectively pick the cookies you want to delete.

    For additional information on deleting cookies, visit the following URL:
    http://www.aboutcookies.org/Default.aspx?

    Your Victories

    For decades, Consumer Reports has worked alongside you to improve the quality, safety, and performance of the products and services we use everyday.

    We're proud to showcase our collective achievements in the timeline below.

    Loading

    1941

    An early win to inform consumers on contraception

    Consumer Reports has never shied away from controversy. In 1937, CR published a Report on Contraceptive Materials. When the Post Office banned the publication calling it "unmailable," we sued. Even though it took seven years, a U.S. Appeals Court overruled the ban in 1944.

    1962

    CR exposes variance in auto insurance rates

    Consumer Reports, in its first report on auto insurance, finds that rates vary by hundreds of dollars and calls for reforms.

    Loading

    1963

    CR tests help support nuclear test-ban treaty

    Concerned about consumer exposure to fallout from nuclear weapons testing, CR conducted several tests in the late 50s and early 60s for the amount of strontium-90 in the North American diet. The surveys showed a potential hazard to consumers and prompted the Federal Government to expand its monitoring of fallout. Our work also helped build public support for the 1963 nuclear test-ban treaty.

    Loading

    1964

    CR warns about the public health threat of cigarettes

    From 1953-1964, Consumer Reports made cigarettes a major target of its criticism with no other product, besides cars, receiving such constant evaluation and scrutiny. CRs independent testing and research culminated in the 1963 publishing of The Consumers Union Report on Smoking and the Public Interest, which sold more than 50,000 copies by November of that year. Our work was used by the Surgeon Generals Advisory Committee on Smoking and Health in drafting the committees 1964 landmark report.

    Loading

    1968

    CR challenges the VA for the right to know about hearing aids

    When we asked the Veterans Administration to release the results of its hearing-aid tests, the V.A. refused, saying that the information was developed exclusively for veterans. Our successful 1968 lawsuit, an early test of the new Freedom of Information Act, challenged the authority of the government to use taxpayers' money to aid just one group of consumers. The first of the test results, which we published in 1971, showed wide variations in the hearing aids' effectiveness.

    Loading

    1968

    US Government mandates safety belts in cars

    In 1956, Consumer Reports tested showed a whopping two-thirds of seat belts failed basic safety and durability tests. The results prompted Consumers Union, long an advocate of safety belts as standard equipment at a time when they were merely an option, to call for better belts and, later, for federal standards that incorporated actual crash testing. CU tested seat belts regularly until the government mandated them in the front seats of all new cars in 1968.

    Loading

    1969

    Toy safety law expanded

    In November 1968, almost a quarter of the electric toys tested by Consumer Reports were judged Not Acceptable because of the risks they posed. These findings contributed to the 1969 passage of the Child Protection and Toy Safety Act, and helped convince the National Commission on Product Safety that mechanical, electrical, and heat hazards should be covered under the law in addition to toys that contain hazardous substances.

    Loading

    1973

    CR testing prompts safer microwaves

    In 1973, Consumer Reports evaluated what was then a new household luxury itemthe microwave. When engineers discovered that the products leaked radiation at levels they could not be sure was harmless, CR issued a not recommended warning for the entire class of products. Based on these test findings, Consumers Union petitioned the government for more stringent standards, ultimately resulting in improved safety features and warning labels on microwaves.

    Loading

    1974

    Fighting for safer drinking water

    In 1974, Consumer Reports published a landmark three-part series on the safety of the United States water supply, contending that water purification systems in many communities had not kept pace with increasing levels of pollution and that many community water supplies may have been contaminated with potential carcinogens. The reports played a role in the enactment by Congress of the Safe Drinking Water Act in December 1974 and prompted inquiries into drinking water safety on local levels.

    1978

    Milk prices in CA reduced

    CU successfully petitioned the California Department of Food and Agriculture to stop enforcement of a statute requiring minimum retail milk prices, saving consumers over $50 million annually in milk expenses.

    1979

    Real estate broker price fixing challenged in CA

    CU petitioned the California Real Estate Commissioner to stop the industry-wide fixing of real estate brokers' commissions in California. CU sponsored a bill, which was enacted, requiring disclosure and notice to consumers that they should negotiate broker commissions.

    1981

    FDA review of safety and efficacy of lindane in shampoo

    The pesticide lindane, banned for use on animals, is approved for prescription use by humans for the treatment of lice and scabies. After hearings, the FDA denied our petition to ban lindane for human use but changed the labeling requirements, substantiallly improving information on how to use the product.

    1981

    Setting new standards for child safety seats

    When CR first crash-tested car seats in 1972 to assess would happen to a small child in a head-on collision, 12 out of 15 of them were rated Not Acceptable. The day after CR released its 1974 report the government proposed a stronger child-restraint standard. As of January 1, 1981, all manufacturers of child safety seats had to certify that their seats would pass a rigorous crash test-- very similar to the one CR used in its own testing and had originally proposed to the government.

    1981

    Agricultural commodity limits challenged

    CU challenged the Federal Marketing Order for naval oranges, which kept prices high in the face of bumper crops by ordering the destruction of half a million tons of oranges. This was the beginning of years of work to eliminate government sactions and limits on the supply of certain commodities. Most supply controls have been ended.

    Loading

    1981

    CR sparks caffeine-free cola wars

    CR analyzed the caffeine content of sodas in 1981 and highlighted the caffeine levels of popular drinks like Coca-Cola, Pepsi, and Mountain Dew. Among the top brands only 7Up and Sprite were caffeine-free. We raised questions about the potential health risks of caffeine to adults as well as children. Shortly after, 7Up launched a new aggressive advertising campaign emphasizing its caffeine-free status, and the cola wars escalated with Coke and Pepsi introducing caffeine-free versions of their sodas.

    Loading

    1982

    CR tests prompt investigation into kerosene heaters

    CR's tests of unvented kerosene heaters in 1982, prompted us to call on the Consumer Product Safety Commission (CPSC) to re-evaluate the health and safety hazards posed by the heaters. The CPSC's eight-month investigation echoed CU's concerns and the industry trade association said it would voluntarily implement the CPSC-recommended changes geared to reduce contact-burn injuries, flare-up fires, and pollutants emitted by the heaters in time for the 1984-85 season.

    1982

    CU vs. American Express

    CU joined Public Advocates representing the National Organization of Women in a suit to end credit policies that discriminated against married women by failing to use community property when assessing credit worthiness. CU prevailed.

    1982

    "Free the Cheese" campaign

    CU petitioned the Reagan Administration to distribute to low-income consumers surplus cheese, non-fat powdered milk, butter and other commodities held by the USDA. The petition was granted and distributions began.

    Loading

    1983

    CR tapped to develop new federal safety standards for lawn mowers

    In the late 1970s, Consumer Reports was selected by the Consumer Product Safety Commission to develop a new safety standard for lawn mowers. Among the safety features recommended by CR that was adopted was a deadman control that had to be held down to allow the blade to turn and that would, when released, stop the blade within three seconds to avoid risk of injury. In 1983, despite a nearly decade-long lobbying battle with the outdoor power equipment industry, the safer mowers hit the market.

    1984

    World Bank petitioned to prevent pesticide abuse in developing countries

    CU partnered with Public Advocates in representing 115 consumer groups from 50 countries asking the World Bank to adopt protocols to restrict the use of loan funds for banned or severely restricted pesticides, and to give funding priority to integrated pest management projects. The World Bank adopted CU's proposed protocols.

    1985

    Deceptive advertising challenged

    CU sued California Federal Savings, then the fourth largest savings and loan company in the nation, for deceptive advertising to promote adjustable rate mortgages. The case was settled for full-page ads in 16 newspapers and an agreement to change future practices.

    1987

    Proposition 65 warnings for toxins established in CA

    CU helped to draft and lead the effort to implement the consumer warning provisions of Proposition 65 in California. Proposition 65 requires the State to publish a list of chemicals known to cause cancer or birth defects or other reproductive harm. This list, which must be updated at least once a year, has grown to include approximately 800 chemicals since it was first published in 1987.

    1988

    Medigap insurance statute enacted

    CU sponsored and secured passage of major California legislation to reform the sale, marketing and benefits of Medicare supplement insurance. The measure was signed into law by the Governor.

    1988

    Community reinvestment by banks promoted

    CU led negotiations with Bank of California as it merged with Union Bank, resulting in an agreement that included free cashing of government assistance checks at all Union Bank branches for two years, $50 million in zero interest loans for affordable housing, and the establishment of multi-family, low-income housing lending goals.

    1988

    Auto insurance reform passed

    CU worked to pass the landmark CA ballot measure called Proposition 103, which required that auto insurers base their rates primarily on three factors: driving record, miles driven, and years of driving experience. It continues to serve as model regulation today in an attempt to roll back laws that allow credit scores rather than driving factors to play a primary role in determining rates.

    1989

    CA Corporate Criminal Liability Act enacted

    CU sponsored and secured passage of a first-in-the-nation bill that subjects corporate managers to criminal liability if they are found responsible for injuries to workers or consumers.

    1990

    Earthquake victims assisted

    CU petitioned the Federal Emergency Management Agency for changes in the manner the agency gives or denies aid to low-income persons displaced by the California Loma Prieta earthquake. FEMA changed some practices.

    1991

    Excessive loan fees challenged

    CU challenged in court how Aetna Financial Services and ITT Consumer Financial Corporation calculated the administrative fees on small, regulated loans. Under the settlement, Aetna was forced to refund two times the overcharge to consumers affected over the prior four years and to stop its former calculation method.

    1991

    Community reinvestment by banks promoted

    CU led negotiations with Bank of America in connection with its merger with Security Pacific National Bank, resulting in an agreement calling for $100 million per year in loans to support low-income housing, with two-thirds of those funds targeted at very low-income consumers, a low-cost checking account, creation of a $3 million revolving loan fund for non-profit intermediary lenders supporting low-income housing, and the purchase of $50 million in low-income housing tax credits.

    1992

    Free copies of checks required

    CU made changes to the California version of the Uniform Commercial Code Articles 3 & 4, governing checks. These improvements were subsequently copied in several other states.

    1992

    Warning on raw milk required

    CU v. Alta Dena Certified Dairy affirmed a judgment requiring a warning label on all raw milk products sold in California.

    1993

    Milk prices reduced

    CU petitioned the California Department of Food and Agriculture to revoke changes made to the milk pricing formula. The petition was granted and a new formula was set, saving consumers $.10-.30 per gallon of milk.

    1993

    Checking accounts protected

    CU defeated legislation that would have permitted a creditor with an interest in a consumer or business checking account to freeze the account for ten days and then to receive the funds, without a court action, unless the account holder went to court to stop the creditor from taking the funds.

    1994

    Food distribution to the poor

    CU supported a campaign that included farmers, churches, and activists, to allow nutritious produce that did not meet cosmetic or size standards to be sold as a low price commodity and distributed to the poor, in defiance of California law that required the fruit to be dumped. As a result, federal and state regulators no longer enforce such laws.

    1994

    Home equity loan abuses challenged

    CU filed suit against Congress Mortgage, a San Francisco area home equity lender accused by elderly and low-income homeowners of stripping the equity from their homes. The CA Department of Corporations suspended Congress Mortgage's license for 10 days and demanded that the lender refund or credit borrowers an estimated $120,000. In addition, an outside monitor was appointed to oversee the activities of the company.

    1994

    Pro-consumer travel industry statute enacted

    CU helped pass a law to regulate the travel services industry in CA. The bill created a victim's restitution fund and a claims process to compensate consumers after a travel company goes out of business.

    Loading

    1994

    Keeping health care companies honest

    CR initiated a fight in California and won to ensure that health care companies converting from non-profit to for-profit statussuch as Blue Cross Blue Shieldused their assets to advance the public interest. We reclaimed $3.3 billion to establish endowments for what are now two of the largest foundations in the country funding countless pro-consumer initiatives. Our Conversion Project spread to 35 states and ultimately delivered $15 billion in endowments to establish pro-consumer foundations.

    1995

    Child safety seat law enacted

    CU sponsored a bill, which was passed and became law in California, that mandated greater public education about California's child safety seat law, and improved statewide programs that offer low-cost and loaner child safety seats to low-income consumers.

    1995

    Deceptive health care sales practices stopped

    CU filed suit to force Foundation Health Plan, one of California's largest health maintenance organizations, to stop marketing its Medi-Cal program door-to-door in San Francisco. The suit also accused Foundation Health Plan of deceptive sales practices. The HMO agreed to cease direct sales in San Francisco.

    1995

    Non-profift advocacy defended

    CU successfully fought off federal legislation which would have forced non-profits to either stop accepting federal grants, or halt altempts to affect public policy, whether by lobbying federal, state and local lawmakers and regulators, through litigation or even by contacting news organizations.

    1995

    Health care conversion legislation enacted

    CU helped pass landmark California legislation to ensure that conversions by non-profit HMOs result in the full transfer of assets to charitable health foundations.

    1996

    Earthquake insurance saved

    CU helped to pass legislation in California that provided for a no-frills earthquake insurance policy that would keep earthquake insurance in the private market and available to California homeowners.

    Loading

    1998

    CR devises a better door lock - and industry acts

    When CR tested door locks in 1998, engineers found that many locks came with the improvements they had recommended eight years prior. Back in 1990, CR found that a simple kick was often enough to defeat a door because of weakness in the strike platethe metal plate that attaches to the door jamb and receives the lock's bolt or latch. For the second round, engineers pro-actively replaced the typical short screws in the strike plate with three-inch screws, which is what they saw in 1998.

    1998

    Debtors' rights safeguarded

    CU secured special consumer provisions in the national uniform law on personal property and secured lending, Uniform Commercial Code Article 9, which affects all 50 states. These provisions protect consumers from pre-creditor changes made elsewhere in the Act. They also require a creditor--for the first time--to inform consumers how the creditor calculated the amount due after repossession before trying to collect.

    1999

    Exposing the dangers of pesticides

    In a first-of-its-kind study of U.S. Department of Agriculture data, Consumer Reports analyzed pesticide levels in 27,000 fruit and vegetable samples and found that many were too high. Not long after the report came out, one of the pesticides we highlighted for its toxicitymethyl parathionwas banned for use on food crops commonly consumed by children.

    1999

    CU exposes problems with reverse mortgages

    CU releases Theres No Place Like Home: The Implication of Reverse Mortgages on Seniors in California. The report recommends specific changes in law and practice, one of which was adopted by Fannie Mae in 2000.

    1999

    CA law protecting stolen debit cards becomes first in nation

    CU wrote and helped to pass California legislation to limit consumer liability for a lost or stolen debit card, making California the first state in the nation with this protection in state law.

    2000

    CU fights for transparency in credit scores for homebuyers

    CU's west coast office is instrumental in enacting a new California law that requires lenders to provide consumers seeking a home loan with their specific credit score and the key reasons why that consumers score was not better. The new law helps consumers better understand how they can improve their credit scores so they can enhance their ability to purchase an affordable home.

    2000

    California's Lemon Law improved

    CU helped pass a law making it easier for consumers who purchased a defective new car to seek relief under California's Lemon Law. The law allowed consumers to seek protection under the law after two repair attempts failed to fix a defect that contributed to an unsafe car.

    2000

    Child safety seat law strengthened

    CU helped pass a California law making it illegal to allow young children (under six years old or sixty pounds) to ride unrestrained by car seats.

    2001

    Cracking down on predatory lending

    CU's west coast office is a key supporter of a new law that offers consumers some important protections against predatory lending. The new law provides protections against some of the most egregious predatory lending practices for homeowners who risk losing their homes and all their equity to costly, unfair, and often illegal mortgage loan practices.

    2001

    Credit card disclosure required

    Following a two-year effort, CU succeeded in passing a new measure that helps consumers understand the true cost of making only the minimum payment for credit card debt. The law requires credit card companies to disclose the total costs and length of time it will take to retire credit card debt of $1000, $2500, and $5000 if the minimum payment is made each month.

    2001

    Mattress flammability standards strengthened

    CU helped secure passage for a California law that raised the flammability standard for polyurethane mattresses and bedding sold in California, creating the strictest mattress flammability standard in the nation.

    2002

    Payday loan oversight established

    CU was the lead consumer group responsible for enacting a California law that established state oversight of the growing payday loan industry, which had been virtually unregulated, requiring payday lenders to abide by annual auditing and reporting rules that apply to other small lenders.

    2002

    Identity theft victims assisted

    CU helped to pass California law that requires consumer credit reporting agencies to provide a victim of identity theft the right to block fraudulent information and to receive a free credit report once a month for up to 12 months, making it easier for victims to monitor credit history in order to limit further damage to their credit record.

    2002

    California sets highest standard in nation for greenhouse gas emissions

    CU worked to pass landmark legislation in CA that set the highest standards for greenhouse gas emissions in the nation.

    2003

    Discriminatory auto insurance rates challenged

    CU filed a petition to the California Department of Insurance to bar insurance companies from basing auto insurance rates mostly on a driver's ZIP code. Basing auto insurance rates on where a driver lives has a disproportionate impact on the poor. The Department held hearings throughout the state and in 2006 found that ZIP code-based rates were unfair and adopted regulations requiring California insurers to base auto insurance premiums primarily on how well a person drives, not on where they live.

    2003

    Ephedra banned in sports supplements

    CU sponsored and helped to pass legislation that banned the California production, sale and distribution of herbal supplements containing the dangerous sports supplement, ephedra, Shortly thereafter, the FDA banned ephedra in sports supplements nationally.

    2003

    Financial privacy protected

    CU was the leading consumer force behind the enactment in California of the countrys toughest financial privacy legislation. This landmark law served as a model for other states and required banks, insurers and other financial institutions to get consumers' consent before sharing their sensitive financial information with most 3rd parties. It also gave consumers the ability to stop the sharing of information with many of a financial institution's affiliated companies.

    2004

    Auto insurance made more affordable for low-income motorists

    CU was lead counsel in a successful challenge of the constitutionality of a California law which allowed insurers to impose large surcharges on millions of previously uninsured motorists. The court ruled that the law was contrary to provisions of Proposition 103, which vests the authority to create new rating factors to the California Insurance Commissioner. This surcharge would have hit low-income Californians the hardest by creating another financial barrier to obtaining insurance.

    2004

    Insurance redlining secrecy challenged

    CU was lead counsel in a redlining suit filed against five of California's largest insurers to challenge the secrecy of specific documents the insurers maintained should not be disclosed because they contained trade secrets. The California Supreme Court rejected that argument and ruled unanimously that the state must disclose the data it had collected from insurance companies to determine whether insurers are discriminating against consumers based on their race, income, or their ZIP code.

    2005

    Exposing food industry marketing to children

    CU publishes Out of Balance, a report documenting how the amount of money spent to advertise foods including soda, candy, snacks and fast foods, dwarfs the dollars spent to promote the California and Federal 5 A Day programs to encourage eating vegetables and fruits. The report concludes that this imbalance is one of the key factors contributing to unhealthful dietary trends in the United States that have led to the obesity crisis.

    2006

    Unfair auto insurance rates barred

    In response to a petition filed by CU, the CA Dept of Insurance held a series of field hearings on the impact of auto insurance rates based primarily on ZIP code. The department found that ZIP code-based rates were unfair and adopted regulations requiring California's insurers to base automobile insurance premiums primarily on how well one drives, not on where one lives.

    2006

    Secrecy ended over recalls for tainted meat

    CU led the fight to pass a California law allowing public health officers to tell consumers the retailers that received USDA-recalled meat and poultry, so they could better protect themselves from food-borne illnesses. The legislation was necessary because the CA Dept of Health Services had signed a secrecy agreement in 2002 with U.S. Dept of Agriculture, agreeing not to release the names of the stores and restaurants where tainted USDA-recalled beef and poultry had been shipped and sold.

    2007

    Food safety laws in states defended

    CU helped defeat the federal National Uniformity for Food Act, an industry-driven measure which would have barred states from addressing food-borne hazards, leaving food safety oversight solely to the FDA, and seriously weakening states' efforts to protect consumers.

    2007

    Comprehensive health reform undertaken in California

    Tapped as the lead consumer group by then-Governor Arnold Schwarzenegger, we helped organize a broad coalition of consumer, senior, labor and other groups to shape a bold health-care reform proposal. This effort made it through a special session of the legislature and failed in the final step due to financing concerns. However, it laid the groundwork for federal health reform, and the plan that President Obama adopted was almost identical to the final California proposal.

    Loading

    2007

    CR champions Anti-rollover technology

    In 2007, a new federal standard was announced requiring electronic stability control (anti-rollover technology) in all vehicles by 2012. Two years prior, we made the decision not to recommend any SUV that did not have it as standard. And so despite the 2012 deadline, the marketplace responded much quicker, in part we believe due to manufacturers wanting to maintain our recommended check mark. This capped a twenty year fight to make vehicles, especially SUVs, safer from the risk of rollover.

    2007

    Identity theft protections extended nationally

    After a multi-year effort, CU's Financial Privacy Now campaign helped enact security freeze laws in 39 states and the District of Columbia. A security freeze gives consumers the ability to lock their credit files to prevent identity thieves from opening fraudulent accounts using stolen personal information. In late 2007, the three major U.S. credit bureaus decided to voluntarily extend this identity theft safeguard in the remaining eleven states.

    2007

    Landmark prescription drug safety law signed

    Prompted by revelations that pharmaceutical companies failed to fully disclose the dangerous side effects of certain medications, CU helped pass landmark prescription drug safety legislation in Congress. The cornerstone of the law required most clinical drug trial results to be made public on the Internet so that consumers could know both the good and bad news about medications.

    Loading

    2009

    Safer products, safer kids

    CR fought for passage of the Consumer Product Safety Improvement Act, which dramatically improved safety standards for toys, cribs and other childrens products. The new law also established a CR-backed national database for Americans to report and access complaints about dangerous products.

    2009

    Credit card protections enacted

    CU was deeply engaged in helping to draft and pass in Congress the Credit Card Act of 2009, which protects consumers from a number of predatory credit card lending practices that had trapped millions of Americans in debt. The law required banks to abide by a set of rules, including limits on interest rate hikes on existing balances, restrictions on late payment penalties, and protections to help consumers pay their credit card bill on time.

    2010

    Kardashian Kard taken off the market

    Just a few weeks after launching their own prepaid debit card, the Kardashian sisters decided to sever their contract with the bank that issued the high cost Kardashian Kard after being roundly criticized by CU for marketing a prepaid card to teens that was loaded with hidden fees and weak consumer protections.

    2010

    Consumer Financial Protection Bureau created

    CU was a vocal champion of the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau and helped to mobilize support in Congress to create the new financial watchdog as part of the Dodd Frank Wall Street reform bill. The CFPB is an independent agency responsible for overseeing the financial industry with powers to police abusive practices and to enact new protections for consumers. It has been particularly active addressing unfair practices in the mortgage, credit card, and student loan sectors.

    March 2010

    Millions get access to affordable health care

    After decades of fighting for health-care reform, and propelled by heartrending patient stories collected from a summer of driving cross-country, Consumer Reports mobilized to pass the Affordable Care Act in March 2010. The new law gave millions access to affordable health care and barred insurers from denying coverage due to pre-existing conditions or charge different rates based on health history or gender.

    2011

    Bank of America ends debit card fee plans

    Following weeks of protests by CU and consumers, Bank of America dropped its plan to begin charging consumers a monthly $5 debit card fee, showing that consumers can have a big impact on banking industry practices by joining together and making their voices heard.

    2011

    California implementation of the Affordable Care Act a national model

    CU helped push California legislation to implement and improve upon the Affordable Care Act by creating a state-based insurance exchange with authority to negotiate rates and standardize benefit designs. "Covered California" became a national model for successful implementation, enrolling 2 million people with coverage, more than 90% of whom get coverage with significant federal subsidies.

    2011

    California bans BPA in baby bottles and sippy cups

    After five years of effort, CU was instrumental in passing a California ban on the dangerous chemical Bisphenol-A (BPA) in baby bottles and sippy cups, leading to a national ban on BPA in baby bottles and sippy cups by the FDA.

    Loading

    2011

    Fighting for safer hospitals

    When CU started a campaign in 2003 to require hospitals to publicly disclose their infection rates, only two states had laws on the books. But tens of thousands of people were going into hospitals to get well and ending up fatally ill or seriously injured from hospital-acquired infections. Thanks to us and our patient advocates, 30 states have passed laws and the federal government now mandates national reporting. Hospital infection rates of have seen declines since the reporting began.

    Loading

    January 2011

    Food Safety Modernization Act

    In the wake of a series of deadly food contamination outbreaks and recalls, Consumers Union was one of several key groups that fought to pass the Food Safety Modernization Act, the first major overhaul of food safety rules in more than 70 years. CR experts were at the table shaping the new law, including a provision empowering the FDA with recall authority over food for the the first time.

    Loading

    November 2011

    Rejection of AT&T/T-Mobile merger

    CR came out in opposition to a proposed AT&T / T-Mobile merger, arguing the it would cause serious harm to competition and higher prices for consumers. We testified before Congress against the proposal, met with federal regulators to make our case, and used CR comparisons of wireless plans to show how the merger would hurt consumers. The deal was ultimately rejected by the FCC.

    2012

    California Homeowners Bill of Rights passed

    CU led the charge to enact a Homeowners Bill of Rights in California, a new law providing borrowers with the tools to avoid a foreclosure through more notification, an opportunity to apply for a loan modification or other relief, and restrictions on dual tracking. The law is credited with causing a significant drop in foreclosures in California, at one time the state with the highest foreclosure rate in the country, and served as a model for similar laws subsequently passed in other states.

    August 2012

    More fuel efficient cars

    The federal government announces a new landmark fuel efficiency standard: 54.5 mpg by 2025. Armed with data from auto testing and consumer surveys, CR was a vocal advocate for the new standard.

    Loading

    July 2013

    Limits on arsenic in apple juice

    In the summer of 2013, the FDA proposed a limit for arsenic in apple juice after Consumer Reports tests revealed around 10 percent of samples had total arsenic levels that exceeded federal drinking-water standards. Most of that arsenic was inorganic arsenic, a known carcinogen. In the wake of the agencys announcement, then head Dr. Margaret Hamburg told The New York Times that the new level was an effort to respond to concerns of consumers after the Consumer Reports investigation.

    Loading

    July 2013

    BPA banned in baby products

    In 1999, CR was one of the first groups to test food packaging for BPA, an endocrine-disrupting chemical used in some food packaging and hard plastics. After working to pass bans in several states on BPA use in a variety of products, the FDA finally announced a federal ban on BPA in baby bottles and sippy cups (2012) and infant formula packaging (2013).

    2014

    Reverse mortgage protections gained in California

    CU was instrumental in the passage of a first-in-the-nation California law to protect reverse mortgage borrowers. This law required reverse mortgage sellers to give prospective borrowers a worksheet to be completed by the borrower before a mandatory counseling session.

    Loading

    January 2014

    CR tests raise concerns over soda carcinogen

    In January 2014, CR unveiled the results of a test of 4-methylimidazole (4-MeI) in soft drinks. Varying levels of 4-MeI, a potentially carcinogenic chemical byproduct of the production of certain types of caramel color, were found in all of the tested samples that listed caramel color as an ingredient. In the wake of our test results and analysis, the Food and Drug Administration announced that it would further study 4-MeI in food. A re-test of several sodas in 2015 showed levels had dropped.

    2014

    Major market actors move away for selling chicken raised with antibiotics

    After a two-year campaign targeting Trader Joe's for selling meat raised with antibiotics, Trader Joe's shifted 90% of its chicken offerings to Raised Without Antibiotics, and major U.S. chicken producers (Perdue, Tyson, Foster Farms, Pilgrim's) and major U.S. chicken purveyors (MacDonald's, Chick-fil-A, Costco, Panera Bread, Subway) announced that they would end the sale of chicken raised with medically-important antibiotics.

    Loading

    March 2014

    A win on backup cameras

    After years of advocating for better visibility standards to address the problem of blind zones behind cars and trucks, the Department of Transportation finally required backup cameras in all vehicles less than 10,000 pounds by 2018. Although Congress mandated this action in 2008, the new rules were repeatedly delayed until CR joined with other safety advocates to file a lawsuit to force the agency to act. The DOTs decision came out one day before the case was scheduled to be heard in court.

    2015

    California passes first-in-nation law to restrict antibiotics in meat production.

    After two years of intense lobbying, consumers scored a victory with a new law in California restricting the use of antibiotics in meat productiona first in the nation. It will go into effect in January of 2018 and prohibits the routine feeding of antibiotics to healthy food animals, including for the purposes of preventing disease or promoting growth.

    2015

    Strong net neutrality rules adopted by the FCC

    CU worked with a broad coalition of organizations around the country to convince the Federal Communications Commission to adopt strong net neutrality rules to keep the Internet free and open. The new rules ensure that Internet users can access the websites, apps, and other online content they choose, without their Internet service providers playing gatekeepers that charge some sites for special treatment, while saddling other sites with slower speeds and second-class status.

    Loading

    April 2015

    Comcast/Time Warner merger blocked

    CU was a vocal opponent of a proposed merger between Comcast and Time Warner Cable, pointing to higher prices, fewer choices and worse customer service for consumers if the two companies combined. We helped mobilize the public to speak out against the deal, generating more than one million petitions and comments filed with the FCC and tens of thousands of phone calls made to regulators. After 14 months of deliberation, the FCC rejected the merger.
